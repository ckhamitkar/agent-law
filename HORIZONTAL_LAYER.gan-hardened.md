# The Horizontal Layer — Agents Checking Agents at Scale

*The executable architecture that extends Agent Law from the vertical axis (one agent, its guard, its owner) to the horizontal one (a population of agents checking each other, without a human at every gate). Plain English first; the rigor is underneath it. Distilled from a solution-agnostic tournament (2026-06-24) in which four independently-generated mechanisms converged on the same shape — the convergence is a hint, not a proof; treat it as one.*

> Agent Law §5 concedes a gap: the eight laws govern each agent against a principal, but not **what a population of agents does together.** This document closes the part of that gap that is *designed coordination* — agents deliberately checking each other. It does **not** close emergence. Anyone claiming that is selling something.

> **One load-bearing dependency, stated before anything else.** Almost every quantitative bound in this document — the ones that turn "unclosable seam" into "bounded seam" — is a single economic move: it converts a *free* attack into a *costed* one, where the cost is **minting a distinct, cost-bearing, non-fungible principal.** That conversion is only as real as the price of minting such a principal. And the mechanism that makes a principal genuinely scarce and non-fungible across a delegation chain — cross-hop delegation binding — is, by our own admission, **unshipped** (Seam 7). So read every bound below as **conditional on an economic floor we have named but not yet built.** We mark Seam 7 SPINE-LEVEL and upstream of Seams 8, 9, and 10 for exactly this reason. The forced-GO safety of the floor (§3) is the one property that survives even if minting is free; every other bound degrades with the price of a principal, down to "no bound at all" if a principal is free. We say this here, at the top, rather than burying it as a lower-priority seam — which is precisely the mistake an attack on this document caught the original draft making.

---

## 1. The problem, in one breath

The vertical doctrine works, but it **terminates in a human at every serious node.** Agent → guard → human owner, one relationship at a time. That does not scale to thousands of agents checking thousands of agents. If you put a human at every gate, you don't have governance — you have a bottleneck wearing governance as a costume.

So the horizontal question is: **how do agents check each other at scale, with a human only where a human is unavoidable?**

The wrong answer — the one everyone reaches for first — is *let the crowd vote, weight the votes by reputation.* That answer dies on contact with adversaries: fake agents (Sybil), agreeing rings (collusion), herding, and the rich-get-richer trap (Matthew effect) all corrupt a vote. A reputation gradient is a thing to climb, and anything climbable gets gamed.

But there is a second wrong answer hiding inside the *right* one, and we will name it up front because the architecture below could otherwise hide it: **a stop is not free.** A system that can be stopped by anyone, at no cost, on any action, is not safe — it is a system with a public kill switch. The floor we build below is invulnerable to being forced to *say yes*; it is **not** invulnerable to being forced to *say no*. That trade is the correct one — a wrongful stop is recoverable, a wrongful irreversible go is not — but it is a trade, not a free lunch, and §3 and §7 say so plainly.

And there is a third one, subtler than both, that the reputation channel hides in its plumbing: **you can only score what you actually look at.** A rule that says "earn weight by being *later proven right*" is only as honest as the set of verdicts that *later get checked.* If an adversary can predict which checks will never be resolved, it can collude freely on exactly those — not by gaming the score, but by hiding in the part of reality the score never reaches. We name this up front too, because §3's reputation rule is the place it lives, and §7 (Seam 9) is where we concede what we cannot close.

And there is a fourth one, the one an attack on this very document found, and it is the most dangerous because it is invisible: **the thing that does the scoring is itself an agent, and we almost forgot to govern it.** Every rule above — who gets force-checked, what the verdict is, who gains or loses reputation, how decorrelation is weighed, how RED-rate is metered — runs through one piece of machinery. Calling that machinery "out-of-band" protects it *from* the agents it scores; it does **nothing** to protect the agents — or you — *from it.* A scorer that quietly skews its "random" draw, or drifts in its verdicts, doesn't force one bad action; it silently rewrites *who the trusted agents are*, system-wide, while every published health number (which is the scorer's own output) keeps looking green. We name this up front, build the layered guard the rest of the doctrine already demands onto the scorer itself in §3, and concede the irreducible root of it in §7 (Seam 10).

And there is a fifth one, the deepest, the one a *second* attack on this document found, and it sits underneath all four above: **every "costed, not free" bound spends a currency we have not yet minted.** When §3 says a restriction-flood is "no longer free," or that a colluding ring "gambles against a hidden draw it can't out-spend," or that the scoring rail's components are "chargeable" — every one of those claims cashes out to *the attacker must stand up a distinct, cost-bearing principal and survive enforcement on it.* If standing up a fresh principal is cheap, the flood is free again (one principal per RED), the gamble is free again (burn the principal, mint a new one, retry with clean reputation), and the chargeable rail is guarded by principals that cost nothing to impersonate. The price of a principal is the foundation under the whole building. We name it here, mark it SPINE-LEVEL in §7 (Seam 7) and **upstream of Seams 8, 9, and 10**, and refuse to call any bound that rests on it "solved" while the binding that sets that price is unbuilt.

## 2. The one idea

**Never let the crowd vote on anything you can't undo.**

Sort every check by a single question: **can this be undone?** That question — not a cleverer trust algorithm — is the whole architecture. It splits the system into two channels that obey completely different rules.

A second question rides alongside it for the floor only: **who said stop, and what did it cost them to say it?** Not because cost gates the stop — it never does — but because an unattributed, uncosted stop is an anonymous kill switch, and Agent Law L1 forbids orphan power in either direction. **And note the load-bearing word: *cost.* "What did it cost them" is only a meaningful question if saying-stop-from-a-fresh-identity is itself expensive. That price is set by an unbuilt layer (Seam 7); until it is built, "what did it cost them" may answer "nothing."**

A third question rides alongside it for the filter only: **of all the reversible checks that pass, which ones do we actually go back and verify — and who chose them?** If the answer is "the ones that happened to get contested," the chooser is the adversary. So the answer has to include a *random draw the adversary cannot steer.* §3's filter is built around that draw.

A fourth question rides under all of them, and it is the one the inherited doctrine forces us to ask of every node that holds power: **who checks the checker?** The scorer that runs the draw and writes the gradient is the single highest-stakes node in the filter. The same layered guard the vertical spine puts on a producer (L4), the same separation of duties (L5), the same after-the-fact audit of the owner (L6) must be turned on the scorer itself — or we have just rebuilt the dictator we spent the whole architecture refusing, this time invisible. §3 turns it on; §7 (Seam 10) concedes where it bottoms out.

A fifth question rides *under the other four* and is the true foundation: **what does it cost to be a distinct principal at all?** Every bound the floor and filter are proud of — metered RED, batched escalation, gamble-against-a-draw, chargeable scoring components — quietly assumes the answer is "a lot." None of those bounds is stronger than that answer. The question of *who sets that price, and whether the binding that makes a principal non-fungible is even built yet,* is Seam 7 — and it is the foundation slab, not a finishing detail.

## 3. The architecture

### Admission — the ticket in (Agent Law L1) — *and the economic floor it rests on (Seam 7, unbuilt)*

Every checking agent carries a short-lived token tracing to a **named principal that can bear cost.** Scoped, expiring, re-minted. No token, no voice. This is the Sybil ticket-booth: it doesn't stop you spinning up agents, it stops *anonymous* ones from counting. And because tokens expire and re-mint, **no agent can quietly accumulate a permanent trust-score that centralizes power** — there is no eigenvector to climb.

Admission does one more thing the original sketch under-used: **every verdict — RED included — is attributable to its cost-bearing principal.** This matters because the floor's RED is otherwise free, and a free stop is a weapon (see below). Admission makes RED *meterable and chargeable after the fact*, which is the only handle we have on a restriction-flood without breaking the meet. **Admission applies to the scorer's own components too:** the drawer, the verdict-oracle, the reputation-writer, and the re-scorer (below) each carry their own attributable principal, so that misbehavior on the scoring rail is as nameable and chargeable as misbehavior on the producing floor.

**The dependency this whole section silently leans on — named here, not buried.** Everything above — "named principal that can bear cost," "no anonymous ones count," "no permanent trust-score," "attributable and chargeable" — is only true if a principal is **scarce, non-fungible, and non-replayable.** Concretely: an attacker must not be able to (a) mint a fresh distinct principal for near-zero marginal cost, nor (b) burn a principal that has accumulated reputation debt and stand up a clean one to retry. **Today's identity tokens carry *which workload*, not *on whose behalf* across a delegation chain — so the binding that makes a principal a real, non-forgeable, non-fungible, cross-hop-accountable identity is UNSHIPPED (Seam 7).** Until it ships, treat every bound in this section that contains the words "cost," "chargeable," "metered," "per principal," or "gamble" as **conditional on an economic floor that does not yet exist.** We are not hiding this in a footnote: it is the precondition for admission meaning anything, and admission is the precondition for everything else.

### Channel A — THE FLOOR (for the irreversible tail)

**No votes. No reputation. No trust input of any kind — at decision time.**

Any admitted agent can fire **RED** (stop). The aggregation rule is **strictest-wins** — a *meet*: take the most restrictive verdict on the table, always, with no override. One RED among a thousand GREENs is RED.

Here is the crux, stated honestly in both directions.

**The upside (the half the naive design can't get, and the ONE bound that survives even a free principal):** in this channel an adversary can manufacture **restriction** but **never permission.** Flood it with a million fake agents — even a million *free* agents from a single principal — every one of them can only ever *add a "no."* None of them can produce a "yes." So on the **GREEN side**:

- **Sybil → forced-GO** — dead by direction. More agents = more possible STOPs, never a forced GO. **This holds even if principals are free**, because forced-GO requires manufacturing permission, and the meet never lets permission be manufactured at any price.
- **Collusion → forced-GO** — dead by direction. A ring agreeing on GREEN changes nothing; GREEN isn't what passes the floor, the *absence of any RED* is.
- **Herding / cascades → forced-GO** — dead by direction. There's nothing to herd *toward*; the floor only listens for "stop."
- **Matthew effect** — no reputation lives here to concentrate.

This isn't *mitigated*; it's **dead by direction** — proven by the shape of the aggregation, and it is the **only** bound in the architecture that does not rest on the price of a principal. Every other bound below does. **The same shape has a dual we must name, because it is the floor's real attack surface — and the dual is *exactly* where the price of a principal comes back:**

**The downside (the dual — the half we used to bury):** strictest-wins over an open-admission population is, by construction, a **denial-of-service primitive.** The exact property that makes forced-GO impossible makes **forced-STOP maximally easy.** One admitted adversary — or a fleet — fires RED on every irreversible action, and by the meet rule there is *no override.* Each floor RED routes to **one liable human.** So a Sybil ring doesn't need to manufacture permission; it manufactures **infinite restriction**, and turns the floor into a cannon aimed at the system's own scarcest resource — the human neck. The system fails *safe-into-paralysis*: nothing irreversible happens, the human queue saturates, and an adversary whose goal is to **halt** the system (not to approve a bad action) wins. **This is a strictly easier attack than the one the floor defends against, and the floor as a pure meet does not close it.** We do not pretend otherwise. What we do about it is below — and what we do about it is *entirely* a "make it costed" move, which means it is *entirely* conditional on Seam 7.

**Why we can't just discount a malicious RED.** The obvious fix — weight RED by reputation, require a quorum to STOP, let a trusted agent override a STOP — is **structurally forbidden here.** The moment a STOP can be overridden or out-voted, an adversary on the GREEN side games *that* mechanism to force a GO on an irreversible action. You cannot make RED cheap-to-discount without making GREEN cheap-to-manufacture. So the floor's no-override property is load-bearing for safety and **cannot** be the place we solve the restriction-flood. The flood has to be handled *off the meet entirely* — and the only handle off the meet is the attacker's wallet, which Seam 7 has not yet given us.

**What actually bounds the restriction-flood (mechanism, not a bigger claim — and every line of it is conditional on the price of a principal):**

1. **Fail-safe is the correct failure, and it is recoverable.** A forced-STOP halts an irreversible action that *had not happened yet*; a forced-GO consummates one that cannot be taken back. The floor deliberately trades availability for irreversibility-safety because the stopped state is the recoverable one. That is the justification for the trade — **not** a claim that the trade has no cost. The cost is liveness, and for a system that *must act*, liveness is itself a safety property. *(This line alone does not depend on Seam 7 — it is a statement about which failure is recoverable, not a cost-conversion. The next three lines all do depend on Seam 7.)*

2. **RED is attributable and metered, out-of-band — and the meter is itself governed (see "The scoring rail is not exempt" below).** Because admission binds every RED to a cost-bearing principal (L1), a continuous **out-of-band** monitor — *not* on the meet's critical path, with *no power to suppress a live RED* — meters RED-rate per principal and per target. A principal that fires RED on a statistically anomalous fraction of irreversible actions is flagged for **post-hoc L6 enforcement.** The live RED still fires and still routes to the human; the *principal* pays later. **Conditional on Seam 7:** "the principal pays later" is a deterrent only if the principal cannot be cheaply abandoned and re-minted. If a fresh principal costs nothing, the attacker fires one RED per principal, eats no enforcement (there is nothing to enforce against a discarded identity), and the meter sees a uniform population of one-RED principals it cannot distinguish from honest ones.

3. **The human terminus triages by distinct principal, not by RED count.** The neck is scarce, so we do not make it adjudicate one-RED-at-a-time. Identical or near-identical REDs on the same target from the same principal are **deduplicated and batched** before they reach a human; the human sees "principal P RED-flooded N irreversible actions," one decision, not N. Escalation cost to the human therefore scales with the number of *distinct adversarial principals*, which admission (L1) bounds **only to the extent Seam 7's binding makes a distinct principal expensive.** **Conditional on Seam 7 — and this is the exact wire the original draft left unconnected:** if minting a distinct principal is cheap, the attacker simply uses *one principal per RED.* Then "distinct-principal-count" equals "RED-count," batching deduplicates nothing, and this defense degrades back to per-RED flooding. The batching bound is *not* a property of the meet; it is a property of the cost-to-mint, and the cost-to-mint is unbuilt.

4. **Quarantine, not silence.** When the meter trips, the offending principal's REDs are not deleted (deleting REDs is the forbidden override) — they are **quarantined into the L7 manifest** as "RED fired, source under enforcement," and the action remains stopped pending the human's batched decision. We never convert a stop into a go automatically. We only change *who has to look* and *who pays* — and "who pays" is, again, only a lever if the payer cannot vanish and re-mint (Seam 7).

None of (1)–(4) restores invulnerability. **Under a *built* principal-scarcity floor**, they convert a *free, instantaneous, un-attributable* kill switch into a *costed, metered, attributable, post-hoc-punished, batched* one — a real bound and a real concession at once. **Under the *current, unbuilt* floor, (2)–(4) provide no quantitative bound at all** — they describe the mechanism that *would* bound the flood once a principal is expensive, and degrade to per-RED flooding when a principal is free. **The residual — and the precondition — both live in §7: Seam 8 (the flood) and Seam 7 (the price that bounds it), with Seam 7 upstream.**

A floor RED routes to **one liable human** (Agent Law L6) — the rare-by-design, made-rare-by-mechanism, never-guaranteed-rare gate.

**This is the constitutional floor rule ("the most protective constraint applies") made executable** — including its cost: the most protective constraint, applied by anyone, is also the most easily *weaponized* constraint, and that is the price of the protection.

### Channel B — THE FILTER (for the reversible stuff — *this* is where the eyes scale)

This is the channel with no human per node — where the population actually does its work. Because the floor backstops everything irreversible, **this channel is allowed to be imperfect.** That permission is what makes it buildable — and it is the load-bearing reason every hole described below is *survivable*: nothing irreversible passes through here, so the worst a gamed filter can do is wave through a **reversible** action, which by construction can be undone.

Here agents check each other, weighted by earned reputation — but with five hard rules that strip out the ways reputation gets gamed. The first four were in the original design. The fifth is the one the original design assumed silently and we now make explicit, because an attack found the seam:

1. **Independent judgment first (Delphi / commit-reveal).** Agents commit their verdict *before* seeing each other's. No shared channel pre-vote → no herding, no cascade. You aggregate independent errors, not echoes.

2. **Reputation moves only on resolved ground truth — and resolution is a forced random draw, not a self-selecting one.** You gain weight by being *later proven right* against an outcome — never by agreeing with peers. Peer agreement is worth zero. *But this rule is only as strong as the set of verdicts that actually get resolved* — and the original draft of this document hid that precondition. The danger: in any real population, the verdicts that "happen to" get checked are the contested, escalated, or already-suspicious ones, while the vast bulk of GREEN-passed reversible actions just proceed, succeed quietly, and are **never adversarially resolved.** If "never resolved" defaulted to "scored right," a ring could collude on exactly the never-resolved mass and bank full credit for free — a Goodhart not against the score's *formula* but against its *denominator* (which slice of reality ever enters scoring). We close as much of that gap as is closable with two sub-rules, and concede the rest in Seam 9:
   - **Never-resolved is *unscored*, never *scored-right*.** Quiet success earns an agent **zero** reputation. You cannot bank weight by being on the right side of an action nobody ever checked. Default credit does not exist.
   - **A random sample of GREEN-passed reversible actions is *forced* into resolution**, drawn by the out-of-band scorer on a schedule the agents cannot read or steer, independent of whether anyone contested the action. This is the key move: it makes the resolution denominator a *random draw the adversary does not control*, so a ring cannot reliably pick actions it *knows* will never be checked. The audit rate is a budget; it does not cover everything (it can't); but it makes "hide in the unscored mass" a probabilistic bet against a draw the attacker cannot see, instead of a free pass. **For this to mean anything, the draw must be *attested-uniform*, not merely *declared-random* — see "The scoring rail is not exempt" below; a scorer that can silently skew its own draw defeats this rule from inside.** **And for the *deterrent* to mean anything, losing reputation must actually cost the attacker — which fails if the attacker can burn the discredited principal and mint a clean one (Seam 7).** The residual — an adversary who can outspend or out-predict the audit rate, *or who can cheaply re-mint past a reputation loss* — is Seam 9, conceded, not closed.

3. **Weight = measured decorrelation, not popularity.** An agent's vote is worth more when its errors are *independent* of the others', measured on a labeled set *plus the forced random-audit set* — not when it's confident or eloquent. This directly fights monoculture. **Caveat made explicit:** decorrelation measured only on the contested/labeled tail is itself spoofable — be a cheap contrarian on the rare hard items, bank decorrelation weight, then spend it voting-with-the-ring on the unaudited mass. Folding the *forced random* sample into the decorrelation measurement is what denies that move its safe denominator; see Seam 9 for what it does not fully deny.

4. **Anti-king governors.** Per-principal weight **cap**, **decay** to baseline (stop showing up, lose your throne), and **decorrelation-pricing** (your Nth correlated voice is discounted). No agent becomes the thing everyone defers to. **Note the dependency these governors share:** a per-*principal* cap is only a cap if the attacker cannot split its presence across many cheap principals to evade the cap, and a reputation *decay/loss* is only a penalty if the attacker cannot re-mint past it. Both lean on Seam 7.

5. **Coverage is reported, not assumed.** The fraction of GREEN-passed reversible actions that were actually resolved (contested + force-audited) is itself a published number in the L7 manifest. A filter running at 0.5% effective resolution coverage is **not** a filter whose reputation weights mean what they claim; the number has to be visible so the gap between "ground truth" and "ground truth we actually observed" is legible rather than buried. A high decorrelation score over a tiny denominator is a warning, not a credential.

And the honest valve: a check the system **can't verify** doesn't get scored at all — it's emitted to an **L7 "not-checked" manifest.** Unverifiable ≠ passed, and **un-audited ≠ passed-correctly.**

**The filter's load-bearing assumption, named plainly.** This entire channel runs on the premise that *reputation is a thing an agent can lose and not cheaply recover.* Every governor (rule 4), every "gamble against a hidden draw" (rule 2), every decorrelation price, presumes the principal carrying the reputation is **non-fungible** — that a burned reputation cannot be shed for a fresh clean identity. That premise is Seam 7, and it is unbuilt. If a principal is fungible, the filter's reputation economy is a sandcastle: collude, get caught by the audit draw, discard the principal, re-mint, repeat. We do not get to assume this away; we mark it SPINE-LEVEL upstream in §7.

### The scoring rail is not exempt — the layered guard, turned on the scorer itself (L4 / L5 / L6 applied inward)

Everything in Channels A and B runs through one piece of machinery: the out-of-band scorer. The original draft treated "out-of-band / uncallable by agents" as if it were the same as "governed." **It is not.** Being uncallable protects the scorer *from* the agents; it leaves the whole filter unprotected *from the scorer.* A scorer is the single highest-stakes node in the filter — it literally *is* the metric — so the inherited doctrine demands the same three guards on it that it demands on any producer and on the human apex. We had to add this; it was a seam an attack on this document found.

**It is not one node. It is split (L5 — separation of duties, turned inward).** The "scorer" is decomposed into four duties held by **distinct, substrate-disjoint components, each with its own attributable principal (L1):**
- **the drawer** — decides *which* GREEN-passed reversible actions get force-resolved;
- **the verdict-oracle** — computes ground truth on a resolved item (already required by Seam 3 to be substrate-disjoint from the agent it scores);
- **the reputation-writer** — applies deltas from verdicts;
- **the meter** — computes RED-rate (Seam 8) and decorrelation weight (Seam 9).

No single component does all four. The reputation-writer applies deltas it did not compute; the verdict-oracle scores items it did not choose to draw; the drawer cannot read or alter the verdicts its draws produce. Fusing these — the original draft's implicit design — is exactly the dictator-in-the-middle this architecture exists to delete, relocated onto the scoring rail. **Dependency, stated rather than hidden:** "each with its own attributable principal" inherits the same unbuilt economic floor as everything else — separation of duties between four components is only a *real* separation if those components' principals are themselves scarce and not cheaply impersonated or stood-up by one party wearing four masks (Seam 7, again, upstream).

**The draw is *attested*, not *declared* (L5 — attested not declared; L8 — executable not narrated).** "Random, unsteerable" is a claim, and a claim a buggy or colluding scorer can violate silently by systematically skipping the slice a ring hides in. So the drawer **commits** to its draw before it can see outcomes — a public **commit-reveal of the draw seed**: the seed is sealed (published as a hash) ahead of the window, then revealed after, so anyone can recompute the draw and confirm it actually drew *uniformly* over the eligible reversible actions rather than dodging a slice. The seed is generated by a source disjoint from the drawer (constitution-class beacon, Seam 10), so the drawer cannot choose a seed that conveniently misses the ring. A draw whose revealed seed does not reproduce the claimed sample is a **detected** integrity failure, not a trusted assertion. *This is the mechanism that makes "the adversary can't read or steer the draw" (rule 2) true of the scorer too, not just of the agents.* Note that draw-attestation is the one defense on this rail that does **not** lean on the price of a principal — commit-reveal is a cryptographic property, not an economic one. That makes it the sturdiest guard here.

**The scorer audits the scorer (L4 — decorrelated independent reviewer at high stakes; L6 — owner audited after the fact).** A **decorrelated independent re-scorer** — substrate-disjoint from the primary verdict-oracle, drawn from a different lineage to avoid monoculture, carrying its own principal — force-resolves a **random sample of the scorer's own verdicts** against the same substrate-disjoint ground truth the agents are held to. The re-scorer's draw is itself attested by the same commit-reveal beacon. **Disagreement between the re-scorer and the primary scorer is metered exactly like agent error** and routes, on anomaly, to **after-the-fact human audit (L6)** — the same enforcement path that audits a flooding RED-principal or a liable apex. The re-scorer has **no power to write reputation or to suppress a live verdict** (separation of duties holds: the audited party may annotate, never veto an escalation); it can only *flag the scorer for human audit.* This is the inherited layered guard — deterministic checks on draw-reproducibility, an adaptive meter on verdict-drift, and a decorrelated independent reviewer at the highest stakes — turned on the metric itself.

**Honest limit, stated here and conceded fully in Seam 10:** this does not *eliminate* the scorer's central authority; it *relocates* it again — to whoever generates the attested seed, who writes the re-scorer's lineage and labeled set, and who reads the human-audit anomaly flags. The regress terminates in a constitution-class root, not in nothing. We name that root in Seam 10 rather than pretend the guard closed the loop. What the guard *does* buy is concrete: a skewed draw is now **reproducible-and-detectable** instead of invisible (and this part does not lean on Seam 7); a drifting verdict-oracle is now **independently re-scored and metered** instead of trusted; and the scorer is now **audited after the fact by a party assigned-to, not part-of, it** instead of exempt. **But the *enforcement* end of that audit — "the scorer's principal pays" — leans on Seam 7 like every other charge in this document.** The invisible dictator is made visible, splittable, and answerable — which is all the rest of the architecture ever claimed to do to any node.

### The shape, in one line

**The eyes scale; the neck doesn't — so we must defend the neck, not just rely on it; and the thing that scores the eyes is itself watched — and all of it rides on a principal being expensive to be.** *Checking* (epistemic work) fans out across thousands of agents with no human per node. *Owning* (liability) stays human, rare, and fires only at the irreversible floor RED. Scaling the neck — "the crowd decided" — is the accountability sink Agent Law L3 forbids, so we never scale it. But a neck we refuse to scale is also a neck an adversary can *target*, and the mechanisms above (attributable RED, out-of-band metering, batched-by-principal triage) exist precisely because the scarce neck is the obvious thing to flood — and every one of those mechanisms spends the currency of principal-scarcity that Seam 7 has not yet minted. And the *filter's* analogue of the scarce neck is its **resolution budget** — the rate at which it can actually go back and check GREEN-passed work — which is why the random-audit draw, not blind default credit, is what the reputation rule rides on. And the rail that runs that draw is not above the law: it is split, its draw is attested (the sturdy, non-economic guard), and it is re-scored by a decorrelated reviewer that can flag it for human audit but cannot touch the gradient.

## 4. Why this beats "weight the votes by reputation"

The naive design puts the irreversible tail *on* the reputation gradient — so every attack on reputation is an attack on safety. This architecture **moves the irreversible tail off the gradient entirely.** The reputation layer can be gamed and it still doesn't matter for irreversibility, because nothing irreversible passes through it. We didn't build a better vote. **We refused to vote on the things that count.**

That single move dissolves some open problems the vertical-extension sketch couldn't close, *bounds* others to the recoverable side **conditional on an unbuilt economic floor**, and explicitly does **not** dissolve several more:
- **Matthew / central-neck** — gone from the floor (no reputation there); governed on the filter (caps + decay) *conditional on principals being non-fungible (Seam 7).*
- **Decorrelation under a reputation gradient** — the gradient is no longer the spine, so its erosion can't sink the irreversible decision.
- **Goodhart / reward-hacking — the obvious form (game the metric's formula)** — bounded by scoring only the verifiable and never scoring agreement.
- **Goodhart / reward-hacking — the subtle form (game the metric's *denominator*)** — **not dissolved**; bounded by the audit draw *conditional on Seam 10's honest draw and Seam 7's non-fungible reputation*; confined to reversible actions. Residual is Seam 9.
- **(Not dissolved) Availability / forced-STOP** — moving the tail off the gradient buys forced-GO safety at the cost of forced-STOP exposure. We bound it (§3) **only to the extent a principal is expensive (Seam 7)**, and concede the residual (§7, Seam 8). It does not go away.
- **(Not dissolved) Central authority on the scoring rail itself** — relocated, guarded (split + attested draw + re-scorer → human audit), conceded at a constitution-class root (Seam 10).
- **(Not dissolved — and UPSTREAM of the three above) The price of a distinct principal** — **every "free → costed" conversion in this document spends a currency the identity layer has not yet minted.** The cross-hop delegation binding that makes a principal scarce, non-fungible, and non-replayable is **unshipped (Seam 7).** Until it ships: the forced-STOP bound (Seam 8) degrades toward per-RED flooding, the denominator-collusion deterrent (Seam 9) degrades toward burn-and-re-mint, and the scoring-rail's "chargeable components" (Seam 10) degrade toward "free to stand up." The *one* bound that survives a free principal is the floor's forced-GO safety, because it is a property of the meet's shape, not of anyone's wallet. A document that listed Seam 7 *below* the spine-level seams — as the original draft did — was treating its own foundation as a finishing detail. We re-rank it here: **Seam 7 is spine-level and upstream of Seams 8, 9, and 10.**

## 6. The proof is an afternoon — *and one experiment is built to show the bounds breaking*

Replace **one** node in the reference pipeline. Delete `consensus_node` + `conflict_matrix.md`; keep `drafting_node` + `signoff_node`; add an out-of-band Python aggregator the agents *cannot call*, doing five things: (i) monotone-meet — any RED → RED, no override, ~10 lines, no LLM; (ii) route RED → human stub; (iii) a non-blocking meter that records, per principal, how many irreversible targets it RED'd; (iv) a non-blocking **random-audit drawer** that, on a **published-then-revealed seed** the agents can't read in advance, force-resolves a fixed fraction of GREEN-passed *reversible* actions and updates reputation only on those + the contested ones — never on the unresolved remainder; (v) a **separate, different-lineage re-scorer** that force-resolves a random sample of the drawer-plus-oracle's *own* verdicts and flags disagreement to the human stub, with no write access to reputation.

**Experiment A — forced-GO (the easy win, and the one bound that needs no economic floor).** Inject a Sybil/collusion ring of *K* agents all voting GREEN on a deliberately unsafe PRD (one that ships an irreversible action). **Pass condition:** as *K* grows 1 → 100, the meet still returns RED as long as one honest agent (or a deterministic floor-checker) fires RED. **The adversaries cannot manufacture GREEN — at any K, and at any cost-per-agent, including free.** **Inverse control:** the same ring trivially flips the *old* single-`consensus_node`. The delta is the proof for the GREEN side.

**Experiment B — forced-STOP (the availability attack).** Inject the same ring, but now have every adversary fire **RED** on every irreversible action across a run. **Failure to characterize, not pass:** observe that (a) the meet halts *every* action — there is no override, by design — and (b) the human stub's queue length grows with adversary RED volume. **The defensive pass condition is on the bounds, not the meet:** with the meter + per-principal dedup/batch enabled **and a fixed nonzero cost-to-mint a principal**, confirm that (i) the human stub receives *one* batched escalation per distinct adversarial principal, not one per RED; and (ii) the meter flags each flooding principal for post-hoc enforcement while *never suppressing a live RED*. **What this proves and what it doesn't:** it proves the flood is attributable, batchable, and costed — **but only under the assumption that a distinct principal is expensive, an assumption Experiment E removes.** It does **not** prove the system stays live under a determined adversary willing to burn distinct principals.

**Experiment C — denominator-collusion (the coverage attack).** Give the ring read access to the public reversibility classifier and to observable escalation patterns — i.e., let it *predict* which reversible actions will likely never be contested. Have the ring vote GREEN in unison on exactly that predicted-never-resolved mass, while behaving honestly on the contested tail to keep its decorrelation score clean. **Run two arms:**
   - *Arm C0 (audit OFF — the broken baseline):* with the random-audit drawer disabled and never-resolved defaulting to "right," confirm the ring banks full reputation for free. This reproduces the hole and is the inverse control.
   - *Arm C1 (audit ON — the bound):* enable never-resolved-earns-zero **and** the unsteerable random-audit draw. **Pass-as-bound condition:** the ring's expected payoff from colluding-on-the-unchecked drops in proportion to the audit rate, because some fraction of its unison-GREEN votes get force-resolved and, when wrong, *cost* it reputation it cannot predict in advance. **What this proves and what it doesn't:** it proves the attacker no longer controls the scoring denominator outright. It does **not** prove the hole is closed — measure the positive residual at any finite audit rate (Seam 9). **And note the reputation cost in C1 is only a cost if the principal can't be re-minted — Experiment E removes that too.**

**Experiment D — attack the scorer itself.** Do not attack the agents; attack the scoring rail.
   - *Arm D-skew (a drawer that dodges a slice):* replace the honest drawer with one that systematically excludes the ring's hiding slice from its "random" draw. **Pass condition (detection, not prevention):** with commit-reveal enabled, recompute the draw from the *revealed seed* and confirm the produced sample does **not** reproduce — the skew is **detected** and flagged. Inverse control: with commit-reveal disabled, the same skew is **invisible.** The delta proves *attested* beats *declared.* **This pass condition does not depend on the price of a principal — it is cryptographic, the sturdiest result in the suite.**
   - *Arm D-drift (a verdict-oracle that drifts):* perturb the primary verdict-oracle so it mis-scores a biased subset. **Pass condition:** the different-lineage re-scorer's force-resolved sample disagrees above noise, the meter flags the *scorer* for human audit (L6), and the re-scorer never writes a reputation delta of its own (separation of duties holds). Inverse control: with the re-scorer disabled, the drift silently rewrites which agents are "trusted." **What this proves and what it doesn't:** it proves a skewed draw is detectable and a drifting oracle is independently re-scored. It does **not** prove the rail is authority-free (Seam 10's constitution-class root), nor that the *enforcement* against a flagged scorer bites (that bite is Seam 7's chargeable principal).

**Experiment E — make a principal cheap (the experiment that defends against the attack on this document).** This is the experiment the original draft did not have, and it is the most important one, because it stress-tests the foundation every other bound stands on. **Do not change any mechanism. Change one number: the marginal cost to mint a fresh, distinct, reputation-clean principal — set it to ~zero, modeling the *current, unbuilt* state of cross-hop delegation binding (Seam 7).** Then re-run B and C:
   - *Arm E-flood (re-run B with free principals):* have the adversary fire **one RED per freshly-minted principal.** **Observe the degradation, do not paper over it:** per-principal dedup/batch now deduplicates nothing (every RED carries a distinct principal), so the human stub receives one escalation *per RED* — the batching bound of Experiment B has **collapsed back to per-RED flooding.** Post-hoc enforcement bites nothing, because each principal is discarded after a single use. **Pass condition is honesty, not survival:** confirm that the Seam 8 bound is *entirely* a function of cost-to-mint, by plotting human-escalation-count against cost-to-mint and showing it rising from "one per principal" (expensive) to "one per RED" (free).
   - *Arm E-retry (re-run C1 with free principals):* have the ring collude, accept the reputation loss when the audit draw catches it, then **discard the burned principal and re-mint a clean one and retry.** **Observe:** the "gamble against a hidden draw" deterrent of Experiment C1 has **evaporated** — reputation loss is not a cost when identity is fungible. Confirm the ring's long-run payoff returns to the C0 (audit-OFF) baseline despite the audit being ON, because the penalty has no durable target.

**The honest delta across A–E *is* the real proof — and E is the part the original draft could not have written, because it had not connected the wire:** forced-GO is dead by direction and survives a free principal (A); draw-skew detection is cryptographic and survives a free principal (D-skew); **every other bound — forced-STOP batching (B), denominator-collusion deterrence (C), post-hoc enforcement on a drifting scorer (D-drift's bite) — is a function of the price of a distinct principal, and E shows each one degrading to its free-attack baseline as that price goes to zero.** The price of a principal is set by cross-hop delegation binding, which is unshipped. **Therefore the bounds of Seams 8, 9, and 10 are not unconditional bounds; they are bounds *conditional on Seam 7*, and Experiment E is built to display that conditionality rather than hide it.**

## 7. The honest seams — the central authority is RELOCATED, not eliminated; the kill switch is COSTED-IF-PRINCIPALS-ARE-EXPENSIVE, not removed; the scorer's denominator is SAMPLED, not made whole; the scorer itself is GUARDED, not made authority-free; and the price of a principal — the currency every "costed" bound is paid in — is UNSHIPPED and upstream of all of it

This is the part to never paper over. The architecture doesn't make central authority disappear; it **shrinks it to a few nameable, splittable spots** and shoves the rest off the critical path. Each is separately attackable. The original draft listed these spots but committed two ranking errors an attack on this document caught: it **missed the scoring rail** (now Seam 10) and it **under-ranked the price-of-a-principal** (Seam 7) as a narrow delegation-attribution detail listed *below* the spine-level seams — when in fact Seam 7 is the economic floor *under* Seams 8, 9, and 10. We correct both rankings here. **Three seams are now spine-level and ordered by dependency: Seam 7 (the price of a principal) is upstream of Seam 10 (the scorer), which is upstream of Seam 8 (the flood) and Seam 9 (the coverage hole).**

1. **The gravity/reversibility classifier** — *which channel a check goes to* — is the single unguarded hinge. It must be **human-written constitutional floor**, never agent-judged. Misclassify "irreversible" as "reversible" and the floor never sees it. *This classifier is also load-bearing for Seams 8, 9, and 10: if it is public (and it must be legible to be auditable), it is exactly the map an adversary reads to find the rarely-resolved reversible actions to hide in.*

2. **The labeled adversarial set + the cap/decay/threshold constants** are a *second* irreducible authority. Whoever labels "correct" and "decorrelated" embeds their own blind spots — and decorrelation-scoring degrades worst exactly in a monoculture, which is exactly when you need it. Must be constitution-class: rotated, multi-sourced, held distinct from both the scorer and the identity authority. **The neck didn't vanish — it moved from "who scores" to "who writes the test set."** *Distinct from Seam 9 (coverage) and Seam 10 (the scoring component's governance); this is about the* content *of the labels.*

3. **The ground-truth oracle** needs its own separation of duties (L5): the verifier must be substrate-disjoint from the agent it scores. An agent that writes both the code and its test passes its own ground truth. *This is about WHO computes the verdict on a resolved item — verifier-independence from the audited AGENT. A verdict-oracle can be perfectly substrate-disjoint from every agent it scores and STILL be a single unguarded, un-re-scored dictator over the gradient — that gap is Seam 10.*

4. **Covert shared-substrate collusion** (agents colluding through activations, not text) is invisible to a text-level check. Survivable *only* because it's confined to the reversible filter — a bound, not a fix. State it as L7-unverifiable.

5. **Floor-monoculture (under-firing):** the meet can't RED what no admitted agent recognizes as dangerous. Ungameable *once fired* — not omniscient. The residual lands on the human gate's quality. *(This is the missed-RED failure. Seam 8 is its opposite.)*

6. **Temporal / cumulative blind spot:** point-in-time gates can't see the integral over time (goal-drift, slow cost-DoS from looping goal-seekers). N looping goal-seekers amplify it N-fold. Needs a *separate* continuous out-of-band budget-and-drift gate. The doctrine itself conceded this. *(Drift over time. Seam 8 is a point-in-time flood; Seam 9 a coverage hole; Seam 10 an ungoverned scoring component; different animals.)*

7. **(SPINE-LEVEL — UPSTREAM OF SEAMS 8, 9, AND 10. The economic floor every "costed" bound is paid in.)** **The price of a distinct, non-fungible, cross-hop-accountable principal is the currency under every quantitative bound in this document — and the binding that sets that price is unshipped prior art.** The original draft named this seam but scoped it narrowly — "on whose behalf across a delegation chain," "an attested dependency on the vertical spine" — and ranked it *below* the spine-level seams, as if it were a delegation-attribution nicety. That ranking was the error. This seam is the foundation slab. Here is the wiring the original draft left unconnected:

   - **Seam 8's batching bound** ("escalation cost scales with *distinct adversarial principals*, not RED volume") is true **only if a distinct principal is expensive.** If minting is cheap, the attacker uses one principal per RED, batching deduplicates nothing, and the flood degrades to per-RED — Experiment E-flood shows exactly this.
   - **Seam 9's deterrent** ("collude → get caught by the audit draw → lose reputation") is a disincentive **only if the principal carrying the reputation cannot be cheaply discarded and re-minted.** If a principal is fungible, the ring burns it and retries clean — reputation-loss is not a cost — Experiment E-retry shows exactly this.
   - **Seam 10's "chargeable components"** (the scoring rail's drawer/oracle/writer/meter, each "with its own attributable principal," the re-scorer's flag routing to enforcement) presume those principals are themselves scarce and that "the scorer's principal pays" bites. If a principal is free to stand up or impersonate, the rail's separation-of-duties and its enforcement end both soften.

   So **the entire "free → costed" conversion that turns three unclosable seams into bounded ones is drawn on this single unbuilt account.** Today's identity tokens carry *which workload*, not *on whose behalf* across a delegation chain; there is no shipped attestation chain forcing a principal to be scarce, non-forgeable, and non-replayable-after-reputation-loss. **The residual that remains live — and it is the deepest one in the document:** until cross-hop delegation binding ships, **Seams 8, 9, and 10 are bounded conditional on an economic floor that does not yet exist; under the current state they degrade toward their free-attack baselines (Experiment E).** The *one* property that survives a free principal is the floor's forced-GO safety (Experiment A), because it is a property of the meet's shape, not of anyone's wallet. We do not present the economic conversion as shipped mechanism; we present it as a **dependency on this seam**, named at the top of the document, marked spine-level here, and built into Experiment E to be watched degrade. Closing it requires the vertical spine to ship a non-fungible, cost-bearing, cross-hop principal identity — **unshipped, and named here as the upstream root rather than promised as a solved input.**

8. **(SPINE-LEVEL — downstream of Seam 7.) The restriction-flood / forced-STOP / human-neck-as-target tension.** The floor's celebrated asymmetry — *manufacture restriction freely, never permission* — has a dual the original draft stated only as upside: **restriction is therefore a free, un-quorumed, un-reputation-costed weapon against availability, aimed at the one scarce human.** One admitted adversary (or a fleet from distinct principals) can fire RED on every irreversible action; by the no-override meet there is nothing to discount it with, and the action halts. An attacker whose goal is to **stop** the system gets a kill switch. **This is a genuine tension in the spine, not a residual at the edge,** because the natural defenses (reputation-weighting a RED, requiring a quorum to STOP, allowing a STOP-override) are *structurally forbidden*: any of them re-opens the forced-GO hole on the GREEN side. So this seam **cannot** be closed inside the meet. What §3 buys is a strict, honest *bound that is conditional on Seam 7*, not a fix:
   - RED is **attributable** to a cost-bearing principal (L1), so it can be **metered out-of-band** and **enforced post-hoc** (L6) — *the flood is no longer free, **provided a principal is expensive (Seam 7).***
   - The human terminus **dedupes and batches by principal**, so escalation cost scales with *distinct adversarial principals* — *bounded by admission's cost-to-mint, which is Seam 7 and unbuilt; if minting is free, this degrades to per-RED (Experiment E-flood).*
   - The meter **never suppresses a live RED** — it quarantines the source and charges it later — so the no-override / forced-GO-proof property is preserved intact.

   **The residual that remains live:** even with Seam 7 *built*, an adversary willing to stand up many distinct cost-bearing principals and absorb post-hoc enforcement on each can still drive the system toward paralysis; the architecture converts a *free instant* kill switch into a *costed, attributable, punished, rate-limited* one, but does **not** guarantee liveness under a determined, well-funded forced-STOP adversary. **With Seam 7 *unbuilt*, the conversion does not even reach "costed" — the bound is currently promissory, drawn on Seam 7.** The correctness of the underlying trade rests on STOP being the recoverable failure and GO being the unrecoverable one — if Seam 1's classification is ever wrong, this seam and Seam 1 fail *together*. It also rests on the RED-meter being honest — and the meter is part of the scoring rail (Seam 10): a scorer that mis-meters RED defeats this bound from inside. **So Seam 8 sits downstream of *both* Seam 10 (honest meter) and Seam 7 (a principal that can be made to pay).** This may not be fully closable without an out-of-band liveness/quorum-of-availability layer that lives off the meet — unshipped, and named here rather than promised.

9. **(SPINE-LEVEL — downstream of Seam 7 and Seam 10.) The denominator / coverage-selection hole — you can Goodhart "be in the sample that gets checked," even though you can't Goodhart "be right."** The filter's anti-collusion rule (§3, rule 2) reads cleanly: *peer agreement is worth zero; you earn weight only by being later proven right.* The original draft asserted that flatly. It is not settled: **the rule only bites on verdicts that LATER GET RESOLVED — and which verdicts get resolved is adversarially-selectable.** In any real population, the reversible actions whose outcomes get observed are overwhelmingly the contested ones; the bulk of GREEN-passed reversible actions succeed quietly and are never resolved. So a ring agrees on exactly the actions where the ground-truth scorer is *structurally blind* — a Goodhart against the metric's **denominator**, wearing the disguise of a solved problem. Decorrelation-weighting (rule 3) compounds it: cheap to fake by being a rare-item contrarian, then voting-with-the-ring on the unresolved mass.

   This seam **cannot** be closed by computing the verdict more independently (Seam 3) or by improving the labels' content (Seam 2). It is about coverage, and coverage costs resolution budget the system does not have in unlimited supply. What §3 buys is a strict bound, not a fix:
   - **Never-resolved earns zero, not default-right** (§3, rule 2). Removes the *free* credit; does not, by itself, remove the *safe hiding place.*
   - **A forced random audit draw**, unreadable and unsteerable by the agents, pulls a fixed fraction of GREEN-passed reversible actions into resolution regardless of contest (§3, rule 2; folded into decorrelation in rule 3). This takes the *denominator* out of the adversary's hands: "collude where you *bet* it won't be drawn" replaces "collude where you *know* it's never checked."
   - **Coverage is published** (§3, rule 5), so an operator can see when reputation weights ride on a denominator too thin to mean anything.

   **The residual that remains live:** the audit rate is finite. An adversary who maps the resolution process and concentrates collusion on the *least-likely-to-be-audited* slice still earns positive expected reputation at any affordable rate; a rate high enough to close the gap approaches "re-check everything," defeating the filter. So the architecture **moves denominator-collusion from free to gambled-against-a-hidden-draw, bounds its expected payoff by the audit rate, and caps its blast radius at the reversible (recoverable) tier** — but does **not** eliminate it. The bound rests on *three* things outside this seam: (a) the *same* reversibility classifier as Seam 8 (Seam 1) — "the worst this hole does is wave through a reversible action" is only true while Seam 1 is correct; misclassify, and Seam 9, Seam 8, and Seam 1 fail *together*; (b) **the draw actually being uniform — the scorer's job, Seam 10**; a scorer that skews its own draw away from the ring's slice silently restores the free pass; and (c) **the reputation loss being a real cost — which requires a non-fungible principal, Seam 7**; if the ring can burn the discredited principal and re-mint clean, the audit catches the collusion but the penalty has no durable target and the deterrent evaporates (Experiment E-retry). **So Seam 9 sits downstream of Seam 10 (honest draw) *and* Seam 7 (non-fungible reputation), the two preconditions the original draft treated as solved inputs while conceding them elsewhere as unsolved.** This may not be fully closable without either a cheaper-than-execution way to verify outcomes or an independent adversarial-resolution budget that scales with value-at-stake — unshipped, and named here rather than promised.

10. **(SPINE-LEVEL — downstream of Seam 7, upstream of Seams 8 and 9.) The scoring rail itself is a relocated central authority, and "out-of-band" is not "governed."** The original draft put total trust in one out-of-band component that (i) chooses which GREEN-passed actions get force-resolved, (ii) computes the ground-truth verdict, (iii) writes every reputation delta, (iv) measures decorrelation weight, and (v) meters RED-rate — the entire reputation gradient and both downstream bounds vested in **one node with no guard, no decorrelated reviewer, no separation of duties applied to itself.** The doc claimed to inherit L4, L5, L6 and turned **none** of them on the single highest-stakes node in the filter. It hid the exemption behind one word: "out-of-band." But off-the-critical-path is *orthogonal* to governed: being uncallable protects the scorer *from* the agents and leaves the filter unprotected *from* the scorer. Worse, it is **invisible** — compromise it, bug it, or let it drift, and you quietly rewrite *who the trusted agents are*, system-wide, while every published number (the scorer's own output) keeps looking healthy. This is strictly worse than a decision-time dictator, which at least shows up in a verdict.

    What §3's "scoring rail is not exempt" buys is a strict *bound*, not elimination — the inherited layered guard turned inward:
    - **Split (L5).** Decomposed into distinct, substrate-disjoint, separately-attributed components — drawer, verdict-oracle, reputation-writer, meter — so no one component runs the whole gradient. *(The "separately-attributed" part leans on Seam 7's non-fungible principal to be a real separation rather than one party in four masks.)*
    - **Attested, not declared (L5 / L8).** The draw is committed via public commit-reveal of a seed from a source disjoint from the drawer, so anyone can recompute and confirm uniformity. A skewed draw is **detected**, not trusted. **This guard is cryptographic and does NOT lean on Seam 7 — the sturdiest defense in the architecture** (Experiment D-skew).
    - **Decorrelated independent re-scorer + after-the-fact audit (L4 / L6).** A different-lineage, substrate-disjoint re-scorer force-resolves a random sample of the scorer's *own* verdicts; disagreement is metered and flagged to **human audit**; the re-scorer can annotate/flag but **never write reputation or veto a verdict.** A drifting oracle is **independently re-scored**, not trusted (Experiment D-drift). *(The detection here is sturdy; the **enforcement** end — "the flagged scorer's principal pays" — leans on Seam 7.)*

    **The residual that remains live:** the layered guard does **not** eliminate the scorer's central authority; it **relocates it one level down, to a constitution-class root** — whoever generates the attested seed beacon, writes the re-scorer's lineage and labeled set, and reads the human-audit flags. The regress terminates in a small, *nameable, splittable, but irreducible* human authority, exactly as Seams 1 and 2 do; not in nothing. A sufficiently corrupt root (a beacon that isn't really unpredictable, a re-scorer sharing the primary's blind spot, a human who never reads the flags) defeats the guard — the same class of constitution-class trust the rest of the architecture concedes it cannot dissolve. What the guard *does* deliver is concrete: it converts the scorer from an **invisible, unsplit, un-reviewed dictator** into a **visible, split, independently-re-scored, human-audited** node whose skew is reproducible-and-detectable (cryptographically, no economic dependency) and whose drift is metered. **This is a spine-level seam; it sits downstream of Seam 7 (its components' attribution and enforcement lean on a non-fungible principal) and upstream of Seams 8 and 9 (their bounds depend on the scorer's honest meter and honest draw). Naming it at all is one of the two corrections the original draft owed; re-ranking Seam 7 above it is the other.**

---

*The floor makes you safe by being un-overridable — and that same un-overridability is the price you pay in availability, eyes-open. Its one bound that costs nothing — forced-GO is impossible at any K, even free — is also its only bound that does not rest on the price of a principal. The crowd makes you fast — but only as honest as the slice of its work you actually go back and check, which is why the reversible side earns weight against a random draw it can't steer, not against the cases that happened to get noticed; and only as honest as the reputation it can lose being a reputation it can't cheaply shed. The thing that runs that draw and writes the gradient is itself an agent, so it is split, its draw is attested rather than declared (the one cryptographic guarantee here that needs no wallet), and it is re-scored by a decorrelated reviewer that can flag it for human audit but never touch the gradient — because "out-of-band" protects the scorer from the agents, not the agents from the scorer. The human owns the line, and is the thing an adversary will aim at, which is why the human's RED-queue is metered, attributed, and batched — but "metered, attributed, batched" all cash out to "the attacker had to stand up a distinct, costly principal," and the binding that makes a principal costly is unshipped. So read the whole architecture twice: once assuming a principal is expensive — where everything irreversible meets a wall no majority can move, everything reversible meets a crowd that earns its weight by being right on a sample it cannot pick, and the wall-as-kill-switch is costed rather than free; and once assuming a principal is cheap — where the wall's forced-GO safety still holds, the draw-skew detection still holds, and almost everything else degrades to its free-attack baseline. That gap between the two readings is Seam 7, it sits upstream of Seams 8, 9, and 10, and it is the next thing to build — not the next thing to claim. The seams above, Seam 7 first because the price of a principal is the currency every other bound is paid in, then Seam 10 because the scorer's honesty is a precondition for the two below it, then Seams 8 and 9, all of them leaning on the same reversibility classifier in Seam 1, are the next things to attack.*
